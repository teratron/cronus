# Project Wiki

**Version:** 1.0.0
**Status:** Stable
**Layer:** concept

## Overview

A client-facing, office-maintained, living documentation surface for each project — a place the client can browse to understand *what their project is, what has been built, what was decided, and how to use it*, without reading the chat transcript or the work board, and without ever having to write or maintain a word of it. It is the legible window onto an office that otherwise works entirely under the hood: the client gives ideas and requirements; the office does the work; the wiki is how the office keeps the client informed. It is a *projection* of the project's real artifacts (board, decisions, deliverables, memory, code intelligence), regenerated as the project evolves — never a source of truth, never internal engineering detail, and never the agent's own retrieval store.

## 1. Motivation

The office's whole premise is that the client does not manage the work — they bring intent, the professionals deliver. But "you don't manage it" must not mean "you have no idea what's happening." Today the only windows into a project are operational surfaces built for the *doer*: the chat (a linear conversation), the kanban board (tasks and states), the sessions log. A non-technical client should not have to reconstruct "what is my project, and where does it stand" from a stream of task cards and agent chatter.

What is missing is a surface built for the *client*: a browsable, plain-language, always-current description of the project itself — its purpose, what has been produced, the decisions that shaped it, and how to use what was delivered. Three properties make this hard and are exactly what the invariants pin down:

- **It must maintain itself.** If the client (or worse, the agent, by hand, every turn) had to keep it current, it would rot immediately. The office must generate and update it from the project's real artifacts as a by-product of working.
- **It must be true, not plausible.** Documentation generated by a model is one hallucination away from confidently describing a feature that does not exist. The wiki must be *grounded* — derived from and citing the actual board, decisions, deliverables, and ledger facts — not free-composed prose.
- **It must be a view, not a second truth.** If the wiki could be edited and become authoritative, it would diverge from the real project state. It is a projection: change the project and the wiki follows; editing the wiki changes nothing.

A surface with these properties turns "the office works invisibly" into "the office works invisibly *and* I can always see where my project stands" — which is what makes maximum automation trustworthy rather than opaque.

## 2. Constraints & Assumptions

- The wiki is for the **client**, in the client's terms; internal engineering and SDD detail are out of scope for it (they live in the knowledge base and design artifacts respectively).
- The wiki is **generated and curated**, not hand-authored by the client; the office owns its upkeep.
- The wiki is a **projection** of project ground truth; it holds no authoritative data of its own.
- One wiki per office/project; its visibility follows the project's sharing posture (private on-device by default).
- Regeneration is event-driven (on significant project change), not per-turn — it is documentation, not a live ticker.

## 3. Core Invariants

Rules every Layer 2 implementation MUST NOT violate:

- **PW-1 (Client-facing, plain-language):** the wiki explains the project to a non-technical client — purpose, what has been built, key decisions, how to use deliverables — in terms the client understands. It answers "what is my project and where does it stand," for reading, never for managing.
- **PW-2 (Office-maintained, client never curates):** the office authors and updates the wiki as a by-product of doing the work; the client is never required to write or maintain it (consistent with OFF-5 / office-managed boards). The client reads, searches, and may request focus — upkeep is the office's job.
- **PW-3 (Projection, not source-of-truth):** the wiki is a derived view of the project's real artifacts; it MUST NOT be the authoritative copy of anything. It is (re)generated from ground truth, and editing the wiki does not alter the project — changes flow project → wiki, never the reverse (consistent with OVZ-1 / projection-not-source).
- **PW-4 (Grounded & attributed):** every substantive wiki statement derives from a real artifact — a decision record, a work product, a board item, an operational-ledger fact — and links/cites it so the client can drill in. The wiki is generated from ground truth, not free-composed; an unattributed claim is a defect, not content.
- **PW-5 (Living & freshness-honest):** the wiki updates as the project changes and reflects current state; sections that have fallen behind their source are flagged stale rather than shown as current, and a visible change history lets the client see what is new since their last visit. A wiki silently diverged from project state is a defect.
- **PW-6 (Navigable & searchable):** the wiki is organized into linked pages (overview → areas → detail) the client can browse and search; no important project knowledge sits more than a few clicks from the overview.
- **PW-7 (Scoped & access-controlled):** there is one wiki per office/project; its visibility and sharing follow the resource-sharing/groups model when the project is shared, and the on-device, no-egress posture when it is not.
- **PW-8 (Distinct from agent knowledge and internal artifacts):** the project wiki — client-facing, about *this* project's state — is distinct from the knowledge base (agent-queryable reference input) and from internal design/spec artifacts (developer-facing). It MUST NOT surface internal engineering or SDD detail the client should not see, and it is NOT a retrieval source the agent reasons from.

> L2 specs cannot reach RFC status until all invariants here are addressed in their "Invariant Compliance" section.

## 4. Detailed Design

### 4.1 What the wiki is, and is not

| Surface | Audience | Direction | Authoritative? |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Project wiki** (this spec) | the client | office → client (output) | no — projection (PW-3) |
| Knowledge base | the agent | reference → agent (input) | no — sourced material |
| Design/SDD artifacts | developers | internal | yes for design intent |
| Kanban / chat / sessions | the doer | operational | yes for operational state |

The wiki is the only one of these built *for the client to understand the project*. It reuses the others as sources (PW-4), never replaces them.

### 4.2 Page model

```text
[REFERENCE]
Wiki (per office):
  Overview       — what this project is, in one screen, plain language
  Areas/Features — what has been built, grouped by capability (from board + deliverables)
  Decisions      — key choices and their rationale (from decision records / ledger)
  How-to / Usage — how the client uses what was delivered
  Glossary       — project terms in plain language
  Changelog      — what changed, newest first (PW-5)
Each page: generated content + source citations (PW-4) + freshness marker (PW-5).
```

### 4.3 Generation & freshness

```text
[REFERENCE]
on significant project change (board transition to done, decision recorded,
                               deliverable produced, milestone reached):
    affected_pages := map(change → page)
    regenerate(affected_pages) from current artifacts        // PW-3 projection
    attribute every claim to its source                      // PW-4
    mark unchanged-but-source-moved pages stale              // PW-5
    append a changelog entry
```

Regeneration is event-driven and incremental (only affected pages), so the wiki stays current without per-turn cost. A page whose sources changed but which has not been regenerated is shown with a stale marker, never silently as current.

### 4.4 Placement

The wiki is reached through a dedicated **Wiki** tab in the per-office sidebar (navigation model). It is one click from anywhere, scoped to the active office (PW-7), and read-only to the client (PW-2).

## 5. Drawbacks & Alternatives

- **Generation cost.** Regenerating documentation has a token cost. Mitigated by event-driven, incremental regeneration (§4.3) — only on significant change, only affected pages — not a per-turn render.
- **Hallucination risk.** Model-generated docs can invent. Mitigated structurally by PW-4 (every claim grounded and cited) — the wiki composes from real artifacts rather than free-composing, and an unattributed claim is rejected.
- **Staleness.** A project moves faster than its docs. Mitigated by PW-5 — stale sections are flagged honestly rather than shown as current, so the client is never misled about freshness.
- **Alternative — just let the client read the kanban/chat.** Rejected: those are operational surfaces for the doer; reconstructing "what is my project" from task cards and transcripts is exactly the burden a non-technical client should not carry (PW-1).
- **Alternative — reuse the knowledge base / design wiki.** Rejected: the knowledge base is agent-facing reference input and the design wiki is developer-facing internal detail; neither is a client-facing projection of this project's state, and leaking internal detail to the client violates PW-8.
